How this works

A two-minute read before you trust anything on the screen.

Read this first

This tool tells you where the weather looks promising for a flight tomorrow. That is all it does. It is a starting point for a conversation with yourself, not a clearance to go.

It knows nothing about NOTAMs, airspace, whether a field needs prior permission, runway state, your own currency, or the weather between you and your destination. A green card is an invitation to plan properly, not a reason to skip the planning.

What it actually does

Every airfield within the range you set gets checked against tomorrow's forecast. The cards are then sorted so the most promising weather sits at the top. You set how far to look with the radius slider, and you can start from your home base, Duxford by default, search for somewhere else, or tap to use your current location.

Where the verdict comes from

The coloured verdict on each card comes from a general weather model. That might sound like the weaker option, but for a question about tomorrow it is the stronger one: the model forecasts days ahead and covers the whole of tomorrow evenly. The official aviation forecast, the TAF, usually runs only twenty-four hours or so, so by the time you are looking at tomorrow afternoon, today's TAF often has nothing to say about it. Letting a half-blind forecast set the verdict would do more harm than good.

So the model leads, and the TAF is used as a safety check on top. Where a real TAF does overlap part of tomorrow, the tool reads it and can raise a flag, a thunderstorm, strong gusts, a low cloudbase, poor visibility. It can only ever add caution, never give the all-clear. The source tag on each card tells you which applied:

Model + TAFVerdict from the model, cross-checked against the real aviation forecast for that field.
ModelVerdict from the model alone, where the field issues no TAF.

When the TAF flags something, an amber or red strip appears on the card spelling out what and when. On every card you can also open the raw report to read the full METAR and TAF yourself, in their proper coded form. If you read them fluently, that is the bit to trust over any summary.

The verdicts

Each card carries one of three readings. They describe the forecast, not your abilities, and they are deliberately blunt:

Looks goodThe forecast sits comfortably inside the limits you picked.
MarginalFlyable for some, not for others. Worth a proper look before you decide.
Probably notSomething in the forecast is past sensible limits. Strong gusts, low cloud, poor visibility, or a thunderstorm.

The numbers on each card

Four figures summarise the day. Gust is the strongest gust forecast, in knots. Low cloud is how much of the sky low cloud is expected to fill, as a percentage, so a high number means an overcast, murky day. Vis is the poorest visibility forecast, in kilometres. Rain is the chance of rain, as a percentage. Where a TAF has flagged something sharper, like a specific cloudbase in feet or a thunderstorm, that appears in the warning strip below the figures.

The distance and rough flight time on each card assume a steady cruise, so treat them as planning figures rather than anything precise.

How cautious today?

The three presets, Cautious, Standard and Confident, change where the line sits between a good day and a marginal one. Cautious wants calm and clear. Confident is happy with a bit more wind or a lower cloudbase.

One thing worth knowing: the presets change your appetite for middling days, not your exposure to bad ones. A day with thunderstorms or a very low ceiling reads "Probably not" on all three settings. Confident never dresses up dangerous weather as merely marginal. If you want to set exact limits of your own, "Fine-tune the numbers" under the presets lets you.

If something looks wrong

A card tagged "Model" simply means that field issues no TAF, so there was nothing to cross-check against. That is normal for smaller strips and not a fault. If the whole page won't load, give it a moment and refresh; the forecast services it relies on are free and occasionally busy.
